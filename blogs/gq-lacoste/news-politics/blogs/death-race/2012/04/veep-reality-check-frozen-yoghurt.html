Veep Reality Check: "Frozen Yoghurt"

Reid Cherlin
April 30, 2012 5:00 am
Facebook
Twitter

This week’s reality-checker is Jano Cabrera, a spokesman for Vice President Gore both in the White House and on Gore’s 2000 campaign. Jano is now a crisis-management consultant at Burson Marsteller, all of which means he knows a thing or two about PR stops gone bad. Here’s our Gchat from after the show:

Reid: Hi, Jano. Whadja think?

Jano: Like last week’s episode, I thought there were elements that were true to life and some that weren’t.

Reid: Like what?

Jano: The one that jumped out at me the most was the idea of them interacting with what they referred to on the show as "the normals" (i.e. regular people)

Reid: Right.

Jano: The idea of putting a qualifier in front of people is something that’s very true for politics. What they got wrong...is the term. Typically, we call them "real people." I mean... c’mon. Calling them "normals" is just offensive.

Reid: Ha. Yeah. Good point. That was my experience, too. We even called them "RPs" a lot. And actually—you get so used to your coworkers talking about "real people" that you forget how telling that term actually is (in what it makes you and your colleagues). Also pretty condescending obviously

Jano: Right, it’s rather telling that politicos use the term. It’s more a commentary on them than the normals. I meant "real people." The other thing that jumped out at me was the last-minute idea of hitting the yogurt shop.

Reid: Yeah?

Jano: Those sorts of stops happen, of course. They’re called OTRs, or off-the-record trips. But even those are fully vetted ahead of time. The location, the owner, and yes... probably the full listing of yogurt flavors available. That’s something they got very right. White House staff would certainly have an entire conversation about what flavor their principal should order. And what that selection means. You never want to wind up making a food faux pas a la John Kerry’s cheese steak

Jano: (while you’re typing, i’m going to insert random emoticons just so I can see how they show up in print)

Reid: Ah yes. Swiss! By the way—if I recall correctly, they call these "Off the Record" stops (OTRs as you point out) not because what happens there is off the record—on the contrary—you want the local press to lap up the candidate/principal looking folksy. But they’re off the record in terms of not being publicized ahead of time, so that they don’t have to put up a security cordon/metal detectors like at normal events. (Go nuts with the emoticons. Just remember that when I publish this I can include ones you didn’t actually type)

Jano: Absolutely. That’s the great irony. These so called off the record jaunts are typically the ones that get the most press. Who doesn’t want to call a reporter and say: "Obama. Ray’s Hellburger visit. Going down today. You want to be there."

Reid: Right

Jano: Speaking of which—the interaction between the reporter and the press staff...

pitch perfect.

Reid: Wanted to ask you about that. Can you say more about that?

Jano: Sure. There are big name reporters that are incredibly influential in terms of their coverage—which of course means they can be beneficial for your own career as a spokesman. As that filter between the principal and the fourth estate, you want that relationship to be as strong as possible. But you’re always aware that the door swings both ways. You want that relationship for what it means for you, whereas they want it for what it means for them.

Reid: problem time.

Jano: The show clearly showed that dynamic, in all its painful glory. I wouldn’t characterize myself as a yeller, but the outbursts and the threats that they showed certainly happen. And they happen on both sides. I was always more of a take-it-all-in and then slash their car tires later kind of guy

Reid: Or that’s what you’ll tell your kids. Yeah, that’s the part of the business I found most unsavory. And yes, I thought it was realistic in terms of making promises you can’t keep. Because you pretty much have to.

Jano: Certainly. And inevitably, there are forces beyond your control. You were on the POTUS side, but let me tell you, Mr. Cherlin...life on the Veep side was harder.

Reid: Go on...

Jano: The idea that POTUS could and would often come in and take an event that you had poured a lot of blood, sweat and tears into... that would happen regularly. For us, we would sometimes downplay our planned events at coordination meetings with the West Wing. Why? Make our press events sound too good and you risk the idea of the WW staff saying... "That’s great. Really great. So glad you’re doing that." And then a phone call later, you’re not and they are.

Reid: (What Selena calls Potus Interruptus)

Jano: That was a great line. Also true how excited the Veep was over... a task force. Think about that....Presidents Clinton, Bush and Obama get real news. Welfare reform, a massive taxcut and healthcare reform. The VP? Typically it is a taskforce or that perennial favorite: reinventing government.

Jano: Unless it’s Cheney... he made news in other ways. Dark ways. Kind of like Snape.

Reid: Cue the mood music. Okay, Jano—this was really helpful. We’ll come after you soon for more reax.

Jano: Excellent. Off to enjoy Girls.

Jano: The show.
