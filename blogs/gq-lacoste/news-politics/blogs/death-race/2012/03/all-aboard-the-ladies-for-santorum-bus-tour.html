All Aboard The Ladies-For-Santorum Bus Tour!

Marin Cogan
March 5, 2012 10:23 am
Facebook
Twitter

I had imagined it a bit like U2’s Zoo TV tour for the leading ladies of social conservatism: Marilyn Musgrave (former Congresswoman, current Susan B. Anthony List honcho), Maggie Gallagher (syndicated columnist, ex-chairman of the National Organization for Marriage), and their like-minded ilk, crisscrossing the South in a big blue luxury bus to spread the pro-life gospel of the party’s happy warrior, Rick Santorum, in the final sprint before Super Tuesday. But, as with most rock bands, the reality of touring is a bit different. On Monday, the day before the election, Musgrave was rolling through Georgia in a 45-foot bus with just two other SBA staffers. Sherman’s march, it was not.

The SBA List is one of the country’s leading anti-abortion groups. Their endorsement of Santorum earlier this year was one of the first signs of the conservative movement coalescing around the former Pennsylvania senator, and Musgrave is now doubling down by leading this bus tour of socially conservative women who support him. The tour was only supposed to be in Michigan, but it was apparently successful enough that they decided to continue it through Tennessee and Georgia. Gallagher, who’s now the head of the Culture War Victory Fund, couldn’t make it today because of a scheduling conflict, so the SBA Listers are more or less on their own. But it’s mostly a media tour anyway, and Musgrave says the bus, coated with a huge image of Santorum gazing steely eyed into the distance, is acting as "a rolling billboard" for his candidacy. "It’s quite a visual," she says.

In its local way, the bus tour has been a success. The bus tour has picked up local coverage in Tennessee, in Michigan and in Ohio, where the report noted that "the bus was met with a small group of pro-choice protesters" from the local college.

Selling female voters on Rick Santorum—the guy who said contraception was "not okay," the man who suggested in his book that "radical feminists" had convinced women they needed to seek fulfillment outside of the home—and then claimed his wife wrote that chapter for him—can’t be easy. Mitt Romney has outperformed him among women voters in New Hampshire, South Carolina, Florida, Nevada and Michigan, according to CNN. Santorum won the female vote in Iowa’s caucuses by two points. He seemed to acknowledge that he needed to do better at his primary night speech in Michigan last week, when he opened with a lengthy tribute to his working mother.

But Musgrave insists her sales pitch is going over well—at least, among the women who’ve shown up to their events. She argues that the focus on Santorum’s anti-contraception comments have obscured his laudable points in the debate. "I think the whole story has not been told," Musgrave says. "Rick Santorum is standing up for our religious liberties and I think that’s critically important for women and men to know." And the recent debate over funding for contraception? Musgrave says that’s only made his election more important: "The fact that he has courage to stand on his convictions, I think the American people are wanting a president they can trust, one that is not poll driven, and Rick Santorum has shown he can withstand slings and arrows that come with making a stand."
